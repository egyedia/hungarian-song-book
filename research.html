The Research

In the Community Singing chapter of the book I write: singing makes you happy, and research proves it. Here is the research.

  1. Clift & Hancox (2001). Choir singers report wide-ranging benefits — emotional, social, and physical wellbeing alike.
    doi.org/10.1177/146642400112100409
  2. Kreutz et al. (2004). Singing — not merely listening — raised secretory immunoglobulin A and lifted mood: a measurable immune and emotional boost.
    doi.org/10.1007/s10865-004-0006-9
  3. Pearce, Launay & Dunbar (2015). Singing together bonds a brand-new group faster than other shared activities — the “ice-breaker effect.”
    doi.org/10.1098/rsos.150221
  4. Weinstein et al. (2016). Group singing raised pain thresholds (a marker of endorphin release) and feelings of closeness — and it works even in large groups.
    doi.org/10.1016/j.evolhumbehav.2015.10.002
  5. Coulton et al. (2015). A randomised controlled trial: community singing improved older adults’ mental-health-related quality of life — and proved cost-effective.
    doi.org/10.1192/bjp.bp.113.129908
  6. WHO / Fancourt & Finn (2019). The WHO’s review of over 900 studies: the arts — singing among them — can help prevent illness and promote health across the whole lifespan.
    who.int — Health Evidence Network synthesis report 67
